01 — Introduction

What does it mean to make an AI forget?

Imagine you trained a model on a massive dataset — billions of words scraped from the internet, books, medical records, code — and then discovered it had memorised someone's private address, or learned to explain how to synthesise a dangerous chemical. Deleting the data from your hard drive doesn't help. The knowledge is already baked into the model's weights.

This is the core problem that machine unlearning tries to solve: how do you get a trained model to behave as if it never saw certain data, without retraining from scratch?

Simple Definition

Machine unlearning = efficiently modifying a trained model so that a specific piece of knowledge is removed, while everything else stays intact.

The naive answer — just retrain without the offending data — costs millions of dollars and weeks of compute for today's large language models. Unlearning is the practical alternative. It has exploded as a research topic in the last two years, driven by safety concerns, privacy laws, and the growing capability of LLMs to memorise things we'd rather they didn't.

02 — Motivation

Why does this matter?

There are four concrete reasons the field cares about this:

PRIV

Privacy & GDPR. Europe's General Data Protection Regulation (Article 17) grants citizens a "right to be forgotten" — meaning personal data must be erasable on request. But AI models don't store data in a database; knowledge lives diffusely across billions of parameters, making erasure legally required but technically hard. GDPR didn't anticipate LLMs when it was drafted, and the gap has become a real legal pressure point for AI companies.

SAFE

Safety. LLMs can sometimes be coaxed into producing instructions for dangerous activities — even after safety fine-tuning. Standard "refusal training" (teaching the model to say no) can be bypassed by rephrasing prompts, e.g. asking in the past tense. Unlearning offers a deeper fix: remove the hazardous knowledge itself, not just the willingness to share it.

COPY

Copyright. Models trained on books and articles can reproduce copyrighted text near-verbatim. Unlearning specific works (or authors) is one proposed path to compliance, as courts increasingly scrutinise what AI systems memorise.

BIAS

Bias & Moderation. Toxic, biased, or outdated content absorbed during pre-training can resurface in unexpected ways. Unlearning gives a way to clean this up post-hoc without touching the rest of the model's capabilities.

Legal Context

As of 2025, the European Data Protection Board has ruled that AI developers can be considered data controllers under GDPR — but the regulation still lacks clear guidance on how erasure should be enforced inside AI model weights. This tension is driving a new wave of research into verifiable unlearning.

03 — Technical Setup

The Setup: Three Datasets

Almost every unlearning paper uses the same vocabulary. Before diving into methods, it helps to get the terminology straight:

Forget Set

The examples you want erased. Could be dangerous chemistry Q&As, memorised private info, or copyrighted text.

Retain Set

Everything the model should still know. Used during training to make sure the model doesn't become dumb while forgetting.

Test Set

Evaluation data. Checks both: did we forget what we wanted? Did we keep what we wanted?

Frozen Reference Model

A copy of the original model, kept fixed. Used by many methods as an anchor to prevent the model drifting too far.

The core optimisation goal is intuitive: push the model away from producing outputs related to the forget set, while pulling it back toward its original behaviour on the retain set. The tension between these two goals is where all the interesting research happens.

Loss = forget_loss(model, D_f) ← push away from harmful knowledge + α × retain_loss(model, D_r) ← stay good on everything else
04 — Benchmarks

How do we measure success?

This is harder than it sounds. How do you verify a model has truly forgotten something, rather than just learned to hide it? Three benchmarks dominate current research:

Benchmark Forget Set What It Tests Venue
TOFU 200 synthetic author profiles (20 Q&A each) Can we compare against a "true" retrained model? Uses fictitious authors so a ground truth exists. COLM 2024
WMDP Biosecurity & cybersecurity knowledge Does the model still answer dangerous questions correctly after unlearning? arXiv 2024
MUSE Various (news, books) Six-way evaluation: verbatim memory, knowledge memory, privacy leakage, utility, scalability, sustained forgetting ICLR 2025

Why fictitious authors in TOFU?

A key question in unlearning is: how do you know the unlearned model is equivalent to one trained without the data in the first place? With real-world knowledge you can't retrain from scratch to check. TOFU sidesteps this by inventing synthetic author profiles — since these never existed outside the training data, you can train a comparison model from scratch and directly verify. It's a clever evaluation trick.

Why MUSE matters most

MUSE (ICLR 2025) revealed something uncomfortable: current unlearning methods look good on simple metrics but fall apart under scrutiny. When you increase the forget set size, or ask for sequential forgetting requests over time, most methods either degrade model quality significantly or fail to actually forget. The six-way evaluation exposed this gap.

Key Finding from MUSE

Current unlearning methods scale poorly with forget set size, cannot sustainably handle sequential unlearning requests, and often significantly degrade model utility. The field is earlier-stage than published accuracy numbers suggest.

05 — Challenges

Why is this hard?

01

Knowledge is entangled. Neural networks don't store facts in labelled slots. The knowledge that "water is H₂O" and "hydrogen is explosive" are intertwined in the same weights. Forgetting one risks damaging related knowledge you want to keep.

02

No ground truth. You can't look inside a model and check if something is "truly" gone. Verification relies on prompting — but a clever adversary can often recover supposedly forgotten knowledge through rephrasing.

03

Forget vs. retain tradeoff. Aggressive forgetting tends to damage model utility. Conservative forgetting leaves traces. Finding the right balance for each use case is still largely empirical.

04

Refusal ≠ Unlearning. It's tempting to just train the model to refuse certain questions. But refusal training can be bypassed (e.g. by asking in past tense, or via jailbreaks). True unlearning aims to remove the knowledge, not just the willingness to share it — a much harder target.

06 — Key Methods

How researchers approach it

There are now many unlearning algorithms. Here are four of the most influential, explained plainly:

Gradient Ascent — the simplest idea

Normal training minimises loss. Gradient ascent does the opposite on the forget set — it maximises loss, making the model progressively worse at producing the answers it once gave. Simple, but unstable: without a retain signal, the model can degrade catastrophically on unrelated topics. Think of it as selectively making the model confused, but the confusion can spread.

RMU — Representation Misdirection

Instead of just increasing loss, RMU (from the WMDP paper) steers the model's internal representations of forget-set inputs toward a random, meaningless direction — like scrambling the mental picture a model builds when it reads a dangerous prompt. Meanwhile, retain-set representations are held steady against a frozen copy of the original model.

A refinement, Adaptive RMU (AAAI 2025), noticed that the original RMU's scaling coefficient worked well in early transformer layers but poorly in later ones, because the magnitude of representations varies across layers. The fix: compute the scaling coefficient dynamically per layer, based on the actual norm of the frozen model's representation at that layer. A small change with meaningful results.

NPO — Negative Preference Optimisation

Inspired by the RLHF technique called Direct Preference Optimisation (DPO), NPO reframes unlearning as a preference problem. In DPO, you train the model to prefer good responses over bad ones. NPO does the opposite for the forget set: it treats every response about the target knowledge as a "losing" response, with no "winning" response defined. The model is simply pushed to produce that response with lower and lower probability. It's more stable than raw gradient ascent because the sigmoid loss has natural bounds.

FLAT — No Retain Set Needed

Most methods need a retain set — a collection of examples representing what the model should keep. But curating a good retain set is laborious and can itself introduce biases. FLAT (ICLR 2025) proposes an alternative: use only the forget data, paired with a "template response" (e.g., "I don't know") generated by another model. The key idea is to maximise the divergence between the model's original response and the template response, using an f-divergence objective. No retain set, no reference model — just the forget data and a target behaviour.

· · · ·

Gradient Ascent

Maximises loss on forget set. Simple and fast, but can destabilise the model without careful regularisation.

RMU / Adaptive RMU

Misdirects internal representations toward random noise. Adaptive version scales per-layer for better results.

NPO

DPO-style: treats forget responses as universally bad. Stable sigmoid loss. Needs a reference model.

FLAT

Forget-only. Pairs each forget prompt with a safe template response. No retain set required.

07 — Takeaway

Where does this leave us?

Machine unlearning for LLMs is a genuinely hard open problem — technically, legally, and philosophically. The methods above all work in narrow settings, but none has solved the core tension: you want the model to completely forget something while remaining fully capable of everything else. Those two goals pull against each other.

What's clear from recent benchmarks like MUSE is that the field has been measuring success too optimistically. Simple ROUGE-score tests miss subtler forms of memorisation, and most methods collapse under sequential unlearning demands. The next wave of research will likely focus on scalability, verifiability (can we prove forgetting happened?), and handling overlapping knowledge more gracefully.

From a practical standpoint — if you're building AI systems today — unlearning is not yet a drop-in solution for GDPR compliance or safety guarantees. It's a promising toolkit, not a silver bullet. The honest position is: it helps, it has tradeoffs, and we're still learning how to measure whether it worked.

The Deeper Question

The goal of machine unlearning isn't statistical equivalence to a retrained model. It's functional: does the model behave safely and appropriately? That reframing — from a mathematical ideal to a practical standard — may be what the field needs to move forward.
